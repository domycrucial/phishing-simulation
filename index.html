Scene 4 · The attack chain unfolds

One stolen password — catastrophic consequences

🎙️
Narrator — Scene 4
Once the attacker has Sarah's credentials, the real damage begins. This is where most people do not realise the full scale of what one stolen password can do. First, the attacker immediately logs into Sarah's corporate email and all connected services. They set up silent email forwarding so every message Sarah receives also goes to the attacker. Next, rather than acting immediately, they watch for days or weeks — reading emails, learning writing styles, understanding business processes. Then they start sending phishing messages to Sarah's colleagues from her own real email address. Because it comes from a known contact, colleagues trust it. The attacker also downloads files from cloud storage, potentially installs ransomware, and can commit financial fraud by impersonating Sarah to redirect company payments. The FBI reports this type of attack — called Business Email Compromise — cost companies 2.9 billion dollars in 2023 alone. Watch the six steps reveal themselves.
1
⚡ Immediate account takeover
Attacker logs into corporate email, HR portal, and every SaaS tool using the stolen password. A forwarding rule silently copies all incoming mail to the attacker's address.
2
🕵️ Silent surveillance for weeks
Rather than acting immediately, the attacker reads emails, learns Sarah's writing style, identifies colleagues, ongoing deals, and payment processes — building a perfect impersonation.
3
📤 Lateral movement — infecting colleagues
Phishing emails are sent from Sarah's real account to colleagues and her manager. They trust it completely — it arrives from Sarah's genuine address with her real name.
4
💾 Data exfiltration
Financial reports, payroll data, client contracts, and trade secrets are downloaded from cloud drives. Sarah's finance role means access to the company's most sensitive records.
5
🦠 Ransomware deployment
A "budget spreadsheet" sent from Sarah's account contains a macro that installs ransomware. The company's systems are encrypted. Operations stop. Ransom demand arrives.
6
💸 Business Email Compromise fraud
Posing as Sarah, the attacker emails accounting to change a supplier's bank details. The next payment of $84,000 is wired to the attacker's account in another country.

Scene 5 · Defence & Knowledge Check

How to protect yourself from email phishing

🎙️
Narrator — Scene 5
Now that you have seen how an email phishing attack works from start to finish, here are six actions that would have protected Sarah — and will protect you. First: always inspect the exact sender domain character by character. Look for zeros replacing the letter O, or other lookalike characters. Second: enable Multi-Factor Authentication on every account. Even if your password is stolen, the attacker cannot log in without your second factor. Third: pause before you click. Urgency and fear are manipulation tools — not signs of a genuine emergency. Fourth: check the URL in the browser before entering any password — look for HTTPS and the correct domain. Fifth: report suspicious emails to your IT team immediately. Sixth: use a password manager with unique passwords for every account. After reviewing the tips below, take the three-question knowledge check to test what you have learned.
🔍
Inspect every sender domain
Hover over sender names to see the real email address. Look for character swaps — 0 vs o, rn vs m. Verify before trusting.
🔐
Enable MFA everywhere
Multi-Factor Authentication means a stolen password alone is worthless. The attacker also needs your physical device or one-time code.
⏸️
Pause — urgency is a weapon
Real IT departments do not threaten to delete accounts in 30 minutes. Take a breath, verify through a known contact, then act.
🌐
Check the URL before typing
Look for HTTPS and the exact correct domain. Navigate directly to sites — never use links from emails.
📣
Report suspicious emails
Forward phishing attempts to your IT security team immediately. Reporting protects your whole organisation.
🔑
Unique passwords + password manager
Never reuse passwords. A password manager generates and stores unique credentials for every account.

Scene 5 · Defence & Knowledge Check

How to protect yourself on WhatsApp

🎙️
Narrator — Scene 5
Now that you have seen how a WhatsApp phishing attack works at every level — from the initial message to complete device destruction — here is your complete defence checklist. The single most important action you can take right now is to enable WhatsApp Two-Step Verification. Go to WhatsApp Settings, then Account, then Two-Step Verification, and set a six-digit PIN. This one action would have completely stopped the attacker from registering David's number on their device. Second: never enter your WhatsApp one-time password on any website. That code is only for the official WhatsApp app during registration — nothing else, ever. Third: before clicking any link promising free data, airtime, or prizes, verify directly with the telecom company through their official app or customer service line. Fourth: never install apps from WhatsApp links. Only use the official Play Store or App Store. Fifth: regularly check WhatsApp Linked Devices in settings and log out any unrecognised sessions. Sixth: check your phone's Device Administrator apps in security settings and revoke access from any app you do not recognise. Complete the quiz below to finalise your training.
🔐
Enable Two-Step Verification
WhatsApp Settings → Account → Two-Step Verification. Set a 6-digit PIN. This stops account hijacking even if your OTP is stolen.
🚫
Never enter OTP on websites
Your WhatsApp OTP is ONLY for the official WhatsApp app during registration. No website, promotion, or person should ever ask for it.
🔗
Verify promotions directly
Real telecom offers come via official apps and customer service channels — not WhatsApp messages from contacts.
📱
Only install from official stores
Never install apps from WhatsApp links. Data booster apps, free VPNs, and prize-claim apps sent via WhatsApp are almost always malware.
👁️
Check Linked Devices monthly
WhatsApp → Linked Devices. Log out any unrecognised session immediately. Do this at least once a month.
🛡️
Audit Device Administrator apps
Settings → Security → Device Admin Apps. Only trusted apps like Find My Device should appear. Revoke and delete any unknown entry.
